Le Grand Menu

The full board: this is the special. I will take you through the history of literature, philosophy, theology and politics in these isles, and the international contexts you will need to understand it.

This is a comprehensive programme of study, designed specifically to tell a story and to build holistic knowledge. I prefer to start at the beginning and work onwards from there. However, I am very happy to begin at any point and to take any selection of topics; my one stipulation is that we discuss them in chronological order.

The whole course:

  • Whispers of Albion: The earliest mentions of Britain and Ireland, in their mythic contexts.
  • Caesar’s Conquest of Gaul and his trouble with the Britons.
  • The Druids: Gymnosophists, Presocratics and what they can tell us about the ancient religions of Europe.
  • The Roman Conquest of Britain: what was it like to be a legionnaire, here?
  • Anti-Roman terrorism: Caratacus, Boudicca and the fight for indigenous freedom.
  • Apocalypse Now: Britain in the ruins of Rome.
  • God and the threat from the north: Early-Christianity and the Viking presence in England.
  • Loneliness in Anglo-Saxon Lyric verse: ‘The Wanderer’, ‘The Seafarer’, ‘Deor’.
  • Beowulf (with a possible extension over two or three sessions, as you wish).
  • England at last: Alfred the Great, the Somerset levels and the miraculous defeat of the Norsemen.
  • ‘1066 e Tut Ceo’: The linguistic, cultural and aesthetic significance of the Norman Conquest.
  • The Once and Future King: Arthur from Geoffrey of Monmouth to Malory, chronicle, romance, and national myth.
  • ‘Whan that Aprille’: Chaucer, the invention of English poetry, and the first great fictional characters.
  • Poets and ‘Makkars’ of poems: Mediaeval verse in the court of the Scottish kings.
  • The New Learning: The Renaissance, Humanism, the printing press, and the moment Europe rediscovered itself.
  • The Break with Rome: Henry VIII, Thomas More, and the Reformation that remade English identity.
  • ‘Sweet Thames, Run Softly’: Spenser, Sidney, and the Elizabethan lyric, the invention of the English love poem before Shakespeare took the stage.
  • ‘The Tragicall History’: Marlowe, Jonson, and why the Elizabethan stage terrified the authorities.
  • Shakespeare (a full session, with the option to extend over further sessions as you wish).
  • ‘No Man is an Island’: Donne, Herbert, Marvell, and the Metaphysical Poets, when English verse learned to think.
  • ‘A World Turned Upside Down’: The Civil Wars, regicide, revolution, and the radical sects.
  • Paradise Lost and Leviathan: Milton, Hobbes, and the birth of free speech.
  • ‘After the King Came Back’: Dryden, Rochester, Aphra Behn, and the scandalous rebirth of English culture in the Restoration.
  • ‘Cogito, ergo sum’: Descartes, Spinoza, and the Continental Rationalists, what Europe was thinking while Britain was arguing about experience.
  • Nothing but the Facts: Bacon, Locke, and the rise of Empiricism, the idea that you should actually look at things.
  • ‘To Be is to Be Perceived’: Berkeley, Hume, and what happens when you push Empiricism to its limits.
  • Order and Reason: Pope and the Augustan Age, when Britain tried to become Rome.
  • ‘Travels into Several Remote Nations of the World’: Swift’s Gulliver and the anatomy of human folly.
  • ‘The Novel Arrives’: Defoe, Richardson, Fielding, and Sterne, and the strange new idea that you could tell the truth by making things up.
  • ‘Sublime Terror’: Walpole, Radcliffe, Lewis, and the Gothic imagination, the dark side of the Enlightenment.
  • The Sublime and the Revolution: Romanticism in all its forms.
  • ‘Negative Capability’: Keats, the odes, and the idea that doubt can be beautiful.
  • The Mad Lives of Shelley & Byron.
  • The Great Tradition: Austen, Dickens, the Brontës, Eliot, and the novel as the mirror of a nation.
  • ‘Ring out, wild bells’: Tennyson, In Memoriam, and the Victorian crisis of faith.
  • ‘My Last Duchess’: Browning and the dramatic monologue, the art of the unreliable speaker.
  • The Void Beneath the Parlour: Late Victorian existentialism, Hardy, Hopkins, and the Victorians who lost their faith.
  • ‘I have nothing to declare but my genius’: Oscar Wilde, wit, beauty, and the trials that ended an era.
  • After the Novels: Thomas Hardy as poet more than novelist, why he gave up fiction to tell the truth.
  • ‘Things Fall Apart’: W.B. Yeats, the last Romantic, the first Modernist, and the greatest lyric poet of the twentieth century.
  • ‘Dulce et Decorum Est’: Owen, Sassoon, Rosenberg, the poets who told the truth about the trenches.
  • ‘Moments of Being’: Virginia Woolf, E.M. Forster, and the Bloomsbury Group, the novel as consciousness.
  • ‘The Men of 1914’: Pound, Eliot, Wyndham Lewis, and the uncomfortable politics of the avant-garde.
  • ‘Spain, 1937’: Auden, MacNeice, Spender, and the poets of the long 1930s, when verse took sides.
  • ‘History is a nightmare’: James Joyce, Dublin, and the reinvention of the English sentence.
  • Beckett, Pinter, and the Europeans: how Continental theory invaded the British stage.
  • Making It New: Modernism and the Postmodern, from The Waste Land to the death of the author.
  • ‘What Are Days For?’: Philip Larkin and The Movement, English poetry after the storm.
  • Look Back in Anger: Osborne, Amis, and the Angry Young Men, the revolt against the Establishment.
  • ‘Saved’: Edward Bond and the theatre of violence, the Royal Court, and the play that refused to look away.
  • ‘Crow’ and ‘Ariel’: Ted Hughes, Sylvia Plath, and the violent imagination of the 1960s.
  • ‘Shibboleth’: Heaney, Paterson, Donaghy, and the state of the lyric at the century’s end.
