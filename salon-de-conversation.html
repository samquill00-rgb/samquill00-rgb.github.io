Le Grand Menu

(£250 for the first three sessions. £85 p/h thereafter. Discount on the total if you book the whole year (52 sessions): £4,420, all at £85 each.)

The full board: this is the special. I will take you through the history of literature, philosophy, theology and politics in these isles, and its international contexts. This is a comprehensive programme of study, designed specifically to tell a story and to build fulsome knowledge.

I prefer to start at the beginning and work onwards from there. However, I am very happy to begin at any point and to take any selection of topics; my one stipulation is that we discuss them in chronological order.

The full programme of 52 sessions appears below.

Le Grand Menu

52 sessions, in chronological order

  1. Whispers of Albion: The earliest mentions of Britain and Ireland, in their mythic contexts. Caesar’s Conquest of Gaul and his trouble with Britons.
  2. Druids, Gymnosophists, Presocratics and what they can tell us about the ancient spirituality of Europe.
  3. The Roman Conquest: What was it like to be a legionnaire in Britain?
  4. Anti-Roman terrorism: Caratacus, Boudicca and the fight for indigenous freedom.
  5. Apocalypse Then: Britain in the ruins of Rome.
  6. God and the Norsemen: Early-Christianity and the Vikings in England.
  7. Loneliness in Anglo-Saxon Lyric verse: ‘The Wanderer’, ‘The Seafarer’, ‘Deor’.
  8. Beowulf: The First English Epic
  9. England at last: Alfred the Great, the Somerset Levels and the miraculous birth of a nation.
  10. ‘1066 e Tut Ceo’: The linguistic, cultural and aesthetic significance of the Norman Conquest.
  11. The once and future king: Arthur from Geoffrey of Monmouth to Thomas Malory.
  12. ‘Whan that Aprille’: The strange modernity of Chaucer’s Canterbury Tales.
  13. Poets and ‘Makkars’ of poems: Mediaeval verse in the court of the Scottish kings.
  14. The New Learning: Renaissance Humanism, the printing press, and Europe’s rediscovery.
  15. Breaking with Rome: Henry VIII and his part in the beginnings of the English Revolution.
  16. ‘Sweet Thames, Run Softly’: Spenser, Sidney, and the Elizabethan lyric, the invention of English love before Shakespeare.
  17. Kit Marlowe and Ben Johnson: dangerous men of the early-modern stage.
  18. Shakespeare (a full session, with the option to extend over further sessions, as you wish).
  19. ‘Though Truth and Falsehood be / Near twins, yet Truth a little elder is’: Donne, Herbert, Marvell, and the Metaphysical Poets; verse, learning to think.
  20. When the world was ending in England: The Civil Wars, regicide, revolution, and radical sects.
  21. Paradise Lost and Leviathan: Milton, Hobbes, and metaphysics of free speech.
  22. What now?: Dryden, Rochester, Aphra Behn, and the scandalous rebirth of English culture in the Restoration.
  23. ‘Cogito ergo sum’: Descartes, Spinoza, and the trial of faith in reason.
  24. English Sense: Bacon, Locke, and the rise of Empiricism; or, why we should look at things.
  25. ‘To Be is to Be Perceived’: Berkeley, Hume and how radical Empiricism is conservative.
  26. Sceptical Reason: Neoclassicism, Alexander Pope and the Augustan Age.
  27. Swift’s ‘Modest Proposal’, Gulliver and the anatomy of human folly.
  28. Something Novel: Defoe, Fielding, Sterne, and the invention of fiction.
  29. The first Dark Enlightenment: Horace Walpole and the Gothic.
  30. Sublime Revolution: William Blake as a primer in Romanticism.
  31. ‘Negative Capability’: Keats, his odes, and his failures.
  32. Percy Bysshe Shelley: poetry as prophecy.
  33. ‘Mad, bad and dangerous to know’: Lord Byron, comic philosopher.
  34. The novel’s great moment: Dickens, Eliot, and the crises of the nineteenth century.
  35. Lying art: Robert Browning’s dramatic monologues and his ‘Sordello’.
  36. Victorian and early-Georgian existentialism: Hardy, Hopkins, and faith.
  37. ‘We all assume that Oscar said it’: Oscar Wilde, wit, beauty, fame, arrogance and suffering.
  38. More than his books: Thomas Hardy’s poetry.
  39. ‘In dreams begins responsibility’: W.B. Yeats, Romantic, Modernist, and the greatest lyric poet of the twentieth century.
  40. Doomed youth: Sassoon, Rosenberg, and the harder poems of the Great War.
  41. Dalloway’s flowers: Virginia Woolf and the Bloomsbury Group, the novel as a thinking thing.
  42. ‘The Men of 1914’: Ezra Pound, T. S. Eliot and Wyndham Lewis; fascism and the avant-garde.
  43. ‘Spain, 1937’: Auden, MacNeice, and the poetry of the 30s.
  44. ‘History is a nightmare [...] from which I am trying to awake’: James Joyce, Dublin, and new Word.
  45. Beckett, Pinter, Europe: how Continental theory played on the English stage.
  46. Making It New: Modernism and the Postmodern, from The Waste Land to the death of the author.
  47. ‘What Are Days For?’: Philip Larkin and The Movement; English poetry in revovery.
  48. Look Back in Anger: Osborne, Kingsley Amis, and the Angry Young Men.
  49. Edward Bond, Shakespeare, and the theatre of violence.
  50. ‘Crow’ and ‘Ariel’; Ted Hughes and Sylvia Plath.
  51. Serious poetry in our time: Geoffrey Hill
  52. ‘Shibboleth’: Heaney, Paterson, Donaghy, and the state of the lyric at the century’s end.
