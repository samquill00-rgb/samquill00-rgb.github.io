A Unique Course of General Learning

I have a long-term student for whom I am continuously developing a course of study in the general history, literature, philosophy and theology of the British Isles (including its European and sometimes its global context).

The brief from the parent was: my daughter is newly arrived in England (though she speaks, reads and writes English exceptionally), teach her everything she needs to know about the cultural and intellectual history of the place in order that she is prepared for her future.

We began with the first substantial written record: Caesar’s account of his invasion in the Commentarii de Bello Gallico, and worked onwards from there.

I am able to teach broadly and deeply on all that is required. I believe this course would be a strong, somewhat unique asset in the offering of any agency who chose to promote it. It would particularly appeal to exceptionally-able students.

Some examples of what we have covered (we are just embarking on the 18th Century after roughly a year of lessons): post-Roman Anglo Saxon poetry; the Viking invasion and the founding of England; the tradition of courtly verse in Mediaeval Scots; Chaucer; Arthurian legend; the Renaissance in various forms; early-modern drama; the Reformation and the civil wars (from both the political and theological perspectives); John Milton, Thomas Hobbes and the crisis of free speech.

Essay Prizes

I have, for the past three years, both individually and in classes, helped students to prepare their submissions for the John Locke Institute’s Global Essay Prize.

Preparation involves a combination of teaching around the broader subject of their question (which will be on some philosophical or political matter), recommending reading and studying that reading, and helping them structure their writing for the final submission.
